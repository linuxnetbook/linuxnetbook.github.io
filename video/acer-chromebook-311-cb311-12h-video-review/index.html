Acer Chromebook 311 (CB311-12H / CB311-12HT) Video Review

The 2024 Acer Chromebook 311 has been unveiled as an entry-level contender in the world of Chromebooks. With its 11.6-inch clamshell design, it is aimed at users seeking a basic, reliable device, particularly students or younger users. However, if you're considering this Chromebook for running other Linux-based systems, there's a lot to unpack.

Design and Build

The Acer Chromebook 311 features an all-plastic build with a silver finish. Although robust enough for everyday use, its weight of 1.19 kg is slightly disappointing for those who value portability. The 180-degree hinge design offers durability, while the keyboard and touchpad provide a comfortable typing experience.

Performance

With an Intel N100 processor and 4GB of LPDDR5 RAM - 8GB in some regions - the performance is adequate for everyday tasks and light multitasking. It supports smooth operation for one logged-in user, and even manages multiple tabs if the memory saver is optimized. It has 64GB of eMMC storage, which is modest by modern standards.

Display and Battery Life

The 11.6-inch IPS display is serviceable with an HD resolution of 1366x768, but it falls short of Full HD standards. Brightness levels are decent for indoor use, and there is a touch variant as well. The battery life impresses with its capability to stretch into a second day of general use, and rapid charging allows for quick power-ups.

Connectivity and Features

Connectivity includes USB-C, two USB-A ports, HDMI, Wi-Fi 6E, and Bluetooth 5.2. This covers basic requirements well. The 720p webcam, along with a privacy slider, and decent albeit unremarkable speakers round out the features.

Assessment for Linux Use

Considering the specs and build quality, the Acer Chromebook 311 is fundamentally designed for Chrome OS, with optimization for web-based tasks and Google's suite of services. For users keen on running other Linux distributions, the hardware shows some promise but has potential limitations:

  • Processor and RAM: The Intel N100 processor paired with 4GB of RAM (or 8GB) can handle lightweight Linux distributions but might struggle with more intensive tasks or robust systems like Ubuntu.
  • Storage: 64GB of eMMC storage is restrictive for dual-booting or running heavier applications frequently found in full-fledged Linux systems.
  • Display: While the HD display may serve general purposes, higher resolutions are beneficial for power users on Linux.

Conclusion

The Acer Chromebook 311 suits its purpose as a basic device well, particularly for educational and casual tasks. For Linux enthusiasts, it might be serviceable with lightweight distributions but not without its constraints. An alternative like the Lenovo Flex 3i, mentioned by the reviewer, could yield more flexibility in terms of features and Linux compatibility. As always, your decision should consider the device's intended use and your specific requirements.
